An Exploration Analysis on Space Missions Since 1957

In this project, we will scrape data from nextspaceflight.com for exploratory data analysis. You can check out the complete project and codes here.

According to Wikipedia, space exploration is the use of space technology to explore outer space. The beginning of the space exploration was driven by a "Space Race" between the Soviet Union and the United States during the Cold War. On October 4, 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik, the first artificial satellite to orbit Earth. The United States sent the first American in space on May 5, 1961. The first Moon landing by the American Apollo 11 mission on 20 July 1969 are often taken as landmarks for this initial period.

In this analysis, I will make interactive charts for data visualization and understanding of the space missions since 1957. The dataset used throughout the analysis was scraped from nextspaceflight.com. You can check out the scrapping code and output here.



Preliminary Data Exploration

The dataset contains a total of 6315 rows and 7 columns with no duplicated values and 3990 Null values in the "price column". To start, I cleaned up the data by adding some additional columns (such as "country" and "year") and converting some columns to its respective types (i.e. the "date" column was converted to a datetime object).



Dataset Overview

The graph above represent the number of launches per year from 1957 to 2022. As we can see, 2020, 1967 and 1966 are the years with the highest number of rockets launched with 157, 140, and 136 total of launches, respectively.

This graph represents the number of launches per country over the years. Russian Federation and the USA are the countries with the biggest number of rockets launched since 1957.

The organisation with the biggest number of rockets launched is the RVSN USSR. This organisation was created as a part of the Soviet Armed Forces in 1959. After the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, their launches are attributed to Russian Federation and Kazakhstan.



Distribution of the Status of Missions and Rockets

Analyzing the graph above, we see that more than 80% of the rockets launched have been retired. The reasons why a rocket is retired are diverse, for example, some of them were not profitable.

When it comes to the success of a mission, we have that more than 90% of all the missions are successful.



Map of Launches by Country

To plot the graph, I created a column with all the Alpha codes for every country. The launches made by Sea Launch weren't attributed to any country (and they are not shown in the map) because Sea Launch is a consortium of four companies from Norway, Russia, Ukraine and the United States. It was formed to launch the Zenit family of rockets from the Odyssey floating launch platform located in the Pacific Ocean.

With this type of visualization, we can have a clear understanding of the launches by country. Here, we can infer that the USA and Russia led the space race throughout those years.

The USA and Russia were the countries with the biggest number of launches, and also the countries with the biggest number of failures by mission.



Comparison between the USA and Russia

Now that we know that the USA and Russia were the countries that had the biggest number of missions launched, I decided to analyse which one of them had the most success and failure rates. For that, I created a new dataframe containing the successes and failures in percents for both of the countries.

With this analysis, we observe that, even if the USA is the number 1 in total of launches, Russia has more succeeded missions than the USA.



Summary of the Missions Analyses by Countries and Organisations

A sunburst chart is a great way to analyse hierarchical data. It consists of concentric layers of circles. The chart segments have each one data category.

Since the beginning of the space exploration in 1957, the space has been an aspect of competition between developed nations. The competition began in earnest on August 2, 1955, when the Soviet Union responded to the American announcement four days earlier of intent to launch artificial satellites for the International Geophysical Year, by declaring they would also launch a satellite "in the near future". The Space Race brought pioneering launches of artificial satellites, robotic space probes to the Moon, Venus, and Mars, and human spaceflight in low Earth orbit and ultimately to the Moon.

As time went by, other nations, such as China, France and Japan, started their own space missions, but still nothing compared with the USA, Russia and Kazakhstan (being the last 2 former parts of the Soviet Union). Prominent space organisations are RVSN USSR, Roscosmos and VKS RF for Russia and Kazakhstan; US Air Force, General Dynamics and NASA for the USA; CASC for China; and Arianespace for France.
