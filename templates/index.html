An Exploration Analysis on Space Missions Since 1957

In this project, I scraped data from nextspaceflight.com for exploratory data analysis. You can check out the complete project and codes here.

According to Wikipedia, space exploration is the use of space technology to explore outer space. The beginning of the space exploration was driven by a "Space Race" between the Soviet Union and the United States during the Cold War. On October 4, 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik, the first artificial satellite to orbit Earth. The United States sent the first American in space on May 5, 1961. The first Moon landing by the American Apollo 11 mission on 20 July 1969 are often taken as landmarks for this initial period.

In this analysis, I decided to make interactive charts for data visualization and understanding of the space missions since 1957. The dataset used throughout the analysis was scraped from nextspaceflight.com. You can check out the scrapping code and output here.



Preliminary Data Exploration

The dataset contains a total of 6315 rows and 7 columns with no duplicated values and 3990 Null values in the "price column". To start, I cleaned up the data by adding some additional columns (such as "country" and "year") and converting some columns to its respective types (i.e. the "date" column was converted to a datetime object).



Dataset Overview

The graph above represent the number of launches per year from 1957 to 2022. As we can see, 2020, 1967 and 1966 are the years with the highest number of rockets launched with 157, 140, and 136 total of launches, respectively.

This graph represents the number of launches per country over the years. Russian Federation and the USA are the countries with the biggest number of rockets launched since 1957.

The organisation with the biggest number of rockets launched is the RVSN USSR. This organisation was created as a part of the Soviet Armed Forces in 1959. After the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, their launches are attributed to Russian Federation and Kazakhstan.



Distribution of the Status of Missions and Rockets

Analyzing the graph above, we see that more than 80% of the rockets launched have been retired. The reasons why a rocket is retired are diverse, for example, some of them were not profitable.

When it comes to the success of a mission, we have that more than 90% of all the missions are successful.



Map of Launches by Country

To plot the graph, I created a column with all the Alpha codes for every country. The launches made by Sea Launch weren't attributed to any country (and they are not shown in the map) because Sea Launch is a consortium of four companies from Norway, Russia, Ukraine and the United States. It was formed to launch the Zenit family of rockets from the Odyssey floating launch platform located in the Pacific Ocean.

With this type of visualization, we can have a clear understanding of the launches by country. Here, we can infer that the USA and Russia led the space race throughout those years.

The USA and Russia were the countries with the biggest number of launches, and also the countries with the biggest number of failures by mission.



Comparison between the USA and Russia

Now that we know that the USA and Russia were the countries that had the biggest number of missions launched, I decided to analyse which one of them had the most success and failure rates. For that, I created a new dataframe containing the successes and failures in percents for both of the countries.

With this analysis, we observe that, even if the USA is the number 1 in total of launches, Russia has more succeeded missions than the USA.

Cleaning Data with SQL

I decided to use the beer review data from Kaggle with the intention of cleaning up and making the data more usable in this project.

According to Tableau, data cleaning is the process of fixing or removing incorrect, corrupted, incorrectly formatted, duplicate, or incomplete data within a dataset. Most professionals in the data world think that data cleaning is one of the most important part of a data analysis project, given that the quality of the dataset we use determines the quality of the results and insights we get. Because of that, I decided to keep a part only for data cleaning projects I make.



Beer Dataset

This dataset was downloaded from Kaggle and contains 13 columns and +1.65 million rows. Due to its size, I opened the PowerShell on my computer and used the following code to divide it in 16 Excel files that I could upload to SQL Server: 

# Read parent CSV
$InputFilename = Get-Content 'C:\Users\Zach7\Documents\Renata\Portfolio Projects\Beer Project\beer_reviews.csv'
$OutputFilenamePattern = 'output_file_'
$LineLimit = 100000

# Initialize
$line = 0
$i = 0
$file = 0
$start = 0

# Loop all text lines
while ($line -le $InputFilename.Length) {

# Generate child CSVs
if ($i -eq $LineLimit -Or $line -eq $InputFilename.Length) {
$file++
$Filename = "$OutputFilenamePattern$file.csv"
$InputFilename[$start..($line-1)] | Out-File $Filename -Force
$start = $line;
$i = 0
Write-Host "$Filename"
}

# Increment counters
$i++;
$line++
}

After uploading all files into SQL Server, I checked if the columns had the same format and altered the ones that weren't in the right format. Then, I created one table and populated it with the result of the UNION ALL command applied for all 16 files.

I started the cleaning process by checking for NULL values. In this process, I found out that only 2 columns had NULL values, the brewery_name and the beer_abv.

To fill in the missing values for the brewery_name column, I first check if there was any brewery with the same ID as the missing ones that had a name, if yes, then I could safely assume that the missing value could be filled by the same as the brewery name with the same ID, given the fact that each brewery has its own ID. It wasn't the case for any of the breweries that had a missing name, so I decided to Google the name of the beers and, with that, I found out the brewery that makes them and could fill in the gap.

The beer_abv column had over 4% of NULL values. To fix that, I decided to fill in the missing values with the average of the alcohol by volume (abv) for that beer style. To do that, I made an INNER JOIN with the same table.

Most brews (if not all of them) were reviewed many times by different people. To make the data more useful, I decided to aggregate the data by getting the average scores of each brew and not taking into consideration the profiles that reviewed the brews.

This process resulted in a much smaller table (less than 66 thousand of rows), with data ready to be explored and analyzed. Check out the complete project here.

Linear Regression in Python

How are people's earnings determined? Today I will build a model for predicting people's earnings, analyse the model and try to improve it.

According to GeeksforGeeks, Classification and Regression are two major prediction problems that are usually dealt with in Data mining and machine learning.

Classification is the process of finding or discovering a model or function which helps in separating the data into multiple categorical classes i.e. discrete values. In classification, data is categorized under different labels according to some parameters given in input and then the labels are predicted for the data. The classification process deal with the problems where the data can be divided into binary or multiple discrete labels.

Regression is the process of finding a model or function for distinguishing the data into continuous real values instead of using classes or discrete values. It can also identify the distribution movement depending on the historical data. Because a regression predictive model predicts a quantity, therefore, the skill of the model must be reported as an error in those predictions

Simple Linear Regression

Linear regression is a linear model, e.g. a model that assumes a linear relationship between the input variables (x) and the single output variable (y). More specifically, that y can be calculated from a linear combination of the input variables (x). With that in mind, I'll implement a Linear Regression algorithm with Python and the Scikit-Learn library! I'll start with a simpler linear regression and then expand onto multiple linear regression with the same dataset.

To create the model, I could simply feed the X and y data directly to the linear regression model, but if I use all the data at once, I won't have any data to test the model with numbers that it hasn't seen yet (i.e., out-of-sample data). Evaluate a model created is very important to get a better idea of its performance in the real world.

This splitting data process is easily achieved through the helper train_test_split() method, which accepts the X and y arrays, and a test_size. The test_size is the percentage of the overall data I used for testing the model. The train_test_split function shuffles the rows of the original data and then takes a proportion to make the training dataset and the rest for testing. I declared the random_state as 10. If I run train_test_split without declaring a random_state, the resulting split will (almost) always be different. The reason I set a random state is to tell the function to shuffle the data identically every time in order to make the results replicable. I split the data as 80% for training and 20% for testing the linear regression using the following code:

y = df_data['EARNINGS'].values.reshape(-1, 1)

I am using a uni-variate regression, which is a regression with a single explanatory variable (the S). Explanatory variables are also referred to as features in machine learning terminology. Using the data on S, the linear regression estimates the best possible line to fit the EARNINGS. The Linear Regression formula to predict the Hourly Earnings in 2011 (EARNINGS) using Years of Schooling (S) has the following structure: $$ {EARNINGS = \theta _0 + \theta _1 S} $$

To find the best possible line, the regression will estimate the y-intercept ("theta zero") and the slope ("theta one"). The line's intercept on the y-axis tells us how much EARNING a person would make if the S was 0. The slope tells us how much extra EARNING we get for a 1-year increase in the S.

To start, I created a LinearRegression object that did the work. Then I had to fit the line to the data by using the .fit() method along with the X_train and y_train data. The fitting line is defined by the features and the intercept/slope. In fact, I inspected the intercept and slope by printing the regression.intecept_ and regression.coef_ attributes.

# R-squared

print(regression_l.score(X_test_l, y_test_l))

The results for the intercept/slope can be plugged into the previous formula: $$ {EARNINGS = 1.02890741 + 1.21917798 S} $$ This means that if the Years of Schooling (S) is 0, the estimated EARNINGS is $1.03/h. The slope tells that for every extra year in school (S), the person's earnings increase by $1.22. That means the higher the education, the higher is your hourly payment.

One measure of figuring out how well the model fits the data is by looking at a metric called r-squared. The r-squared for this model comes in at around 0.081 for the training data and 0.072 for the testing data. This means that the model explains about 8% of the variance in EARNINGS for the training data, which is a pretty low r-squared.

Multivariable Linear Regression

To try and make the model more accurate, I decided to run a multivariable regression. It's similar to the simple regression, but instead of using only one variable (S), I can predict the EARNINGS using many variables instead, and this is also a much more common scenario in real life, where many things can affect the EARNINGS result. The new formula for the Linear Regression to predict the Hourly Earnings in 2011 (EARNINGS) using Years of Schooling (S), Total Out-of-School Work Experience (EXP), and Gross Household Income prior to 1997 (HHINC97) is: $$ {EARNINGS = \theta _0 + \theta _1 S + \theta _2 EXP + \theta _3 HHINC97} $$

The results of the heatmap showed that the EARNINGS have a higher correlation with S, EXP, and ASVABMK (mathematic component of the ASVAB). I initially ran a multivariable regression with those 3 values, but the ASVABMK didn't increase the prediction of the model, so I decided to leave it out of the model and use the variables I assumed in the beginning of the project that would be more impactful when predicting the EARNINGS.

Following what has been done with the simple linear regression, after loading and exploring the data, I divided it into features and targets. The results for the intercept/slope can be plugged into the new formula: $$ {EARNINGS = -12.8078 + 1.6089 S + 0.9224 EXP + 0.00004 HHINC97} $$ For the linear regression, the model had 1 variable and 1 coefficient, now in the multiple linear regression model, it has 4 variables and 4 coefficients. The interpretation of the y-intercept is a bit different because it's a negative value this time. Literally, the result of -12.8078 means that if all the other variables are 0, the estimated EARNINGS -$12.81/h, which is clearly unrealistic. The reason is that the model is simply a straight line, and it's returning the best formula to create this line. Considering that it's not possible to have a negative earnings, it's important to be careful when interpreting a simple model too literally. After all, it's just an estimate and this estimate will be the most accurate on the chart where it has the most data points (rather than at the extreme left or right).

Experiment and Investigate Further

I decided to analyse the distribution of the residuals. In particular, I'm interested in the skew and the mean. In an ideal case, the distribution is something close to a normal distribution. A normal distribution has a skewness of 0 and a mean of 0. A skew of 0 means that the distribution is symmetrical - the bell curve is not lopsided or biased to one side.

To transform the results, I could either find a more appropriate model to predict the EARNINGS or transform the data and try to make it fit better with the linear model.
